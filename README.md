<h1>1.Introduction</h1>
Primary conductors carry electrical power at higher voltages from substations to distribution transformers over longer distances. They are designed to handle large loads, minimize line losses, and provide reliable supply across wide geographical areas. Primary distribution typically operates at voltages such as 11 kV, 22 kV, or 33 kV.

On the other hand, secondary conductors operate at lower voltages, carrying electricity from distribution transformers directly to consumers. These conductors supply residential, commercial, and small industrial loads, usually at voltages like 415 V (three-phase) or 230 V (single-phase). Since these lines are closer to consumers, factors such as safety, voltage regulation, and conductor size become critical.

<h2>Conclusion</h2>
Primary and secondary conductors play a crucial role in the efficient operation of electrical distribution and transformer systems. Primary conductors carry high voltage and relatively low current from the power source to the transformer, requiring proper insulation and safety measures. In contrast, secondary conductors operate at lower voltage but carry higher current to supply electrical loads, making conductor sizing, voltage drop, and power loss important considerations.
